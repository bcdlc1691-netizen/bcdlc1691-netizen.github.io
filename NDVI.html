Assignment 3: NDVI

Normalized Difference Vegetation Index

The Sentinel-2 satellites were launched by the ESA and collect enough data to form a new full image of the Earth every 5 days. I used this data to calculate vegetation health with the NDVI color bands. This was done by getting an orthomosaic, a combination of various satellite images (as cloudless as possible for visibility, but the location of my choice proved it slightly complicated) put together to form a single complete picture. For this assignment, we learned how to use Google Earth Engine with JavaScript. The outcome were several maps showing vegetation health and its difference between 2017 and 2025, a timeframe showing changes pre and post pandemic. ROI= east of Tarapoto, San Martín, Peru. NDVI values range from -1 to 1, where higher values indicate healthier, denser vegetation. Read more: https://gisgeography.com/ndvi-normalized-difference-vegetation-index

About vegetation health, NDVI, and relevance

Healthy vegetation (with high concentrations of chlorophyll) reflects more near-infrared and green light compared to other wavelengths. But it absorbs more red and blue light. This is why satellite imagery and NIR go hand in hand when identifying vegetation health indices such as the NDVI. Additionally, studying land cover patterns and health over time may reveal underlying environmental issues and sustainability concerns. Very low NDVIs are representative of water bodies, something that is clearly seen in the green and pink maps. Furthermore, the last map shows the movement of the meandering river, showing us beautiful curls along the way. By observing these patterns riverside populations could prepare accordingly and land zoning could be fine tuned.
